Beginner Level

What real analysis is: calculus, rebuilt on bedrock. Real analysis replaces "gets closer and closer" with airtight ε-definitions, and then proves exactly when the tools of calculus can be trusted — and exhibits the monsters (nowhere-differentiable functions, non-integrable functions) that live just outside the safe zone.

Sequences & Convergence

Intuition: a sequence \((a_n)\) converges to \(L\) if its terms eventually settle into any tolerance band around \(L\) — and stay there forever.

Formal definition:

\[\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L \iff \forall \varepsilon > 0\ \exists N \in \mathbb{N}\ \forall n \geq N: |a_n - L| < \varepsilon\]

The quantifier order is the whole game: the tolerance \(\varepsilon\) is the challenge; the threshold \(N\) is the response, allowed to depend on \(\varepsilon\).

Interactive: the ε-N game. Pick a sequence and an ε: the band \(L \pm \varepsilon\) is shaded, and the computed \(N\) is marked — every dot to the right of it sits inside the band. For \((-1)^n\), no single \(L\) works: convergence fails.

Step-by-step ε-N proof: \(\frac{1}{n} \to 0\)

Step 1: Fix an arbitrary \(\varepsilon > 0\) (the challenge).
Step 2: Choose the response — take \(N = \lceil 1/\varepsilon \rceil + 1\), so that \(N > 1/\varepsilon\).
Step 3: Verify — for every \(n \geq N\): \[|a_n - 0| = \frac{1}{n} \leq \frac{1}{N} < \varepsilon\] Every ε has been answered with a working N, so \(\lim \frac1n = 0\). ∎

(Try it in the widget: set ε = 0.10 and check it marks N = 11.)
Monotone Convergence Theorem: a sequence that is increasing and bounded above converges (to its least upper bound). This is how \(e\) is born: the widget's \((1 + 1/n)^n\) is increasing and bounded above by 3, so it must converge — its limit is defined to be \(e \approx 2.71828\) (see Functions § Exponential).

Exercises:

  1. Prove \(\frac{n}{n+1} \to 1\) with ε-N.
    Solution \(\left|\frac{n}{n+1} - 1\right| = \frac{1}{n+1} < \varepsilon\) whenever \(n > \frac{1}{\varepsilon} - 1\); choose \(N = \lceil 1/\varepsilon \rceil\). ∎
  2. Does \(a_n = \sin(n)\) converge?
    Solution No. Its values are dense in \([-1, 1]\), so the tail keeps visiting points ~1 apart — no band of radius \(< \frac12\) around any L can trap it.

Intermediate Level

Series

A series \(\sum a_n\) converges iff its partial sums \(S_N = \sum_{n=1}^N a_n\) converge as a sequence.

Geometric Picture

A series is a stack of bars of width 1 and height \(a_n\) laid along the n-axis; convergence means the total stacked area is finite. The comparison test: your stack fits under a stack already known finite. The ratio test: eventually each bar is at most \(r < 1\) times the previous — so the stack fits under a geometric staircase.

Interactive: the harmonic race. Compare partial sums of \(\sum 1/n^2\) (converges to \(\pi^2/6 \approx 1.645\)) with the harmonic \(\sum 1/n\) — which crawls, but crawls forever. The bracket marks show the grouping argument: each bundle \([\frac12], [\frac13 + \frac14], [\frac15 + \dots + \frac18], \dots\) contributes at least \(\frac12\).

Step-by-step: the geometric series

Step 1: \(S_N = 1 + r + \dots + r^N\). Multiply by \(r\): \(rS_N = r + \dots + r^{N+1}\).
Step 2: Subtract — everything telescopes: \((1 - r)S_N = 1 - r^{N+1}\), so \(S_N = \frac{1 - r^{N+1}}{1 - r}\).
Step 3: If \(|r| < 1\) then \(r^{N+1} \to 0\): \[\sum_{n=0}^\infty r^n = \frac{1}{1 - r}\]
Step-by-step: harmonic divergence by grouping (the ½-bricks)
\[\underbrace{\tfrac12}_{\geq \frac12} + \underbrace{\tfrac13 + \tfrac14}_{\geq \frac24 = \frac12} + \underbrace{\tfrac15 + \tfrac16 + \tfrac17 + \tfrac18}_{\geq \frac48 = \frac12} + \underbrace{\tfrac19 + \dots + \tfrac1{16}}_{\geq \frac{8}{16} = \frac12} + \dots\] Each group is at least ½ because it has \(2^k\) terms, each at least \(\frac{1}{2^{k+1}}\). Infinitely many ½-bricks → the stack grows past every bound. ∎ (These are exactly the widget's brackets.)
Convergence tests as algorithms:
Comparison: if \(0 \le a_n \le b_n\) and \(\sum b_n\) converges, so does \(\sum a_n\). Worked: \(\frac{1}{n^2 + 7} \le \frac{1}{n^2}\) → converges.
Ratio: if \(\lim |a_{n+1}/a_n| = r < 1\), converges (absolutely); \(r > 1\) diverges; \(r = 1\) says nothing. Worked: \(\sum \frac{2^n}{n!}\): ratio \(\frac{2}{n+1} \to 0 < 1\) → converges (to \(e^2 - 1\)).
Real-World Applications: decimal expansions are geometric series (\(0.999\ldots = \sum 9 \cdot 10^{-n} = 1\)); annuity and mortgage formulas; Zeno's paradox resolved (\(\frac12 + \frac14 + \frac18 + \dots = 1\): infinitely many steps, finite total).

Rigorous Limits & Continuity

The ε-δ machinery from Functions § Continuous, now with teeth. The model proof every student should own:

Step-by-step: \(\lim_{x \to 2} x^2 = 4\), with the bounding trick

Step 1: Factor the error — \(|x^2 - 4| = |x - 2| \cdot |x + 2|\). We control \(|x - 2|\); the nuisance factor \(|x + 2|\) must be tamed.
Step 2: Tame it — first promise \(\delta \le 1\). Then \(|x - 2| < 1\) forces \(x \in (1, 3)\), so \(|x + 2| < 5\).
Step 3: Choose δ — \(\delta = \min\left(1, \frac{\varepsilon}{5}\right)\).
Step 4: Verify — if \(|x - 2| < \delta\): \[|x^2 - 4| = |x - 2||x + 2| < \frac{\varepsilon}{5} \cdot 5 = \varepsilon \;∎\]

Monsters that sharpen the definitions:

  • Dirichlet function \(f(x) = 1\) if \(x\) rational, 0 if irrational — discontinuous everywhere. It cannot be plotted at all: between any two points it takes both values infinitely often; any drawing would be two solid horizontal lines, which is a lie.
  • Thomae's function \(f(p/q) = 1/q\) (lowest terms), \(f(\text{irrational}) = 0\) — continuous at every irrational, discontinuous at every rational. Proof sketch: near any point, fractions with small denominators (the only ones with large \(f\)-values) are rare, so \(f \to 0\) along almost everything.

Uniform vs pointwise continuity: \(f\) is uniformly continuous if one δ works for the whole domain: \(\forall\varepsilon\ \exists\delta\ \forall x, c: |x - c| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(x) - f(c)| < \varepsilon\). The quantifier \(\forall c\) moved after \(\exists\delta\) — that is the entire difference.

Interactive: watch uniformity fail. \(f(x) = 1/x\) on \((0, 1)\): keep ε fixed and slide \(c\) toward 0 — the widget's δ shrinks toward zero. No single δ serves all points, so \(1/x\) is continuous but not uniformly continuous on \((0,1)\). (On \([0.1, 1]\), a compact set, it would be — see below.)

Advanced Level

Compactness, IVT/EVT, and the Riemann Integral

Heine–Borel theorem: in \(\mathbb{R}^n\), a set is compact ⟺ closed and bounded. Compactness is the property that turns "locally controllable" into "globally controllable" — on a compact domain, continuous functions are automatically bounded, attain their extrema (EVT), and are uniformly continuous.

Why \([a, b]\) matters: on the non-compact \((0, 1)\), the continuous \(1/x\) is unbounded (no EVT) and, as the widget above shows, not uniformly continuous. Close the interval and every pathology disappears.

IVT via bisection: the proof of the Intermediate Value Theorem is the bisection algorithm from Numerical Methods: halve the interval keeping the sign change; the nested intervals shrink to a point (completeness of \(\mathbb{R}\)!), and continuity forces \(f = 0\) there. A rare case where the constructive algorithm and the abstract proof are the same object.

The Riemann (Darboux) Integral

For a partition \(P = \{x_0 < \dots < x_n\}\) of \([a,b]\), define the upper and lower sums:

\[U(f, P) = \sum_i \Big(\sup_{[x_{i-1},x_i]} f\Big)\Delta x_i, \qquad L(f, P) = \sum_i \Big(\inf_{[x_{i-1},x_i]} f\Big)\Delta x_i\]

\(f\) is Riemann integrable iff the ceiling and floor can be squeezed together: \(\inf_P U(f,P) = \sup_P L(f,P)\) — equivalently, \(\forall \varepsilon\ \exists P: U(f,P) - L(f,P) < \varepsilon\).

Interactive: squeeze the staircases. Upper rectangles (ceiling) and lower rectangles (floor) for \(f(x) = x^2\); raise \(n\) and watch the gap \(U - L\) go to zero — integrability made visible.

Step-by-step: upper/lower sums for \(f(x) = x\) on \([0, 1]\), n equal parts

Step 1: On \([\frac{i-1}{n}, \frac{i}{n}]\), \(f\) is increasing: \(\inf = \frac{i-1}{n}\), \(\sup = \frac{i}{n}\).
Step 2: Sum: \[L = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{i-1}{n}\cdot\frac1n = \frac{n(n-1)/2}{n^2} = \frac12 - \frac{1}{2n}, \qquad U = \frac12 + \frac{1}{2n}\] Step 3: Squeeze — both → \(\frac12\) as \(n \to \infty\), and \(U - L = \frac1n \to 0\), so \(\int_0^1 x\,dx = \frac12\). ∎
A function with NO Riemann integral: the Dirichlet function on \([0,1]\). Every subinterval contains rationals and irrationals, so every upper sum is 1 and every lower sum is 0 — the gap never closes. The ceiling and floor staircases are permanently 1 apart. This failure is the doorway to the research level below.

Exercises:

  1. Show a monotone function on \([a,b]\) is always Riemann integrable.
    Solution For equal partitions, \(U - L = \frac{b-a}{n}|f(b) - f(a)|\) (the staircase gaps telescope) → 0. ∎
  2. Why does EVT fail for \(f(x) = x\) on \((0, 1)\)?
    Solution The candidates for extrema (0 and 1) are not in the open interval — sup and inf exist but are never attained. Compactness (closedness) is exactly what guarantees attainment.

Research Level

Lebesgue Integration

Riemann slices the domain; Lebesgue slices the range: "for each height, how much of the domain sits at that height?" — where "how much" is measure. The rationals have measure zero (cover the \(n\)-th rational by an interval of length \(\varepsilon/2^n\): total \(< \varepsilon\)), so the Dirichlet function, hopeless for Riemann, integrates trivially to 0. Lebesgue's integral also swaps limits and integrals under far weaker hypotheses (dominated convergence theorem) — the foundation of modern probability and PDE theory.

Baire Category

The Baire category theorem says a complete metric space is never a countable union of nowhere-dense sets. Its strange consequence: most continuous functions (in the category sense) are nowhere differentiable — the Weierstrass monster is the rule, not the exception. Smoothness, which calculus treats as normal, is topologically rare.
