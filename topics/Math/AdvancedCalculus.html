Chapter 0 — Introduction (Logic, Sets, Functions)

The set-theoretic and logical substratum on which everything is built: quantifiers, connectives, sets, relations, functions, Cartesian products, duality, Boolean operations, and equivalence relations. The single most important habit to acquire here is reading and negating strings of quantifiers — every later definition of limit, continuity, compactness, and convergence is such a string.

Key Concepts

  1. Quantifiers & negation. \((\forall x)P(x)\), \((\exists x)P(x)\); order matters — \((\exists y)(\forall x)P\) is stronger than \((\forall x)(\exists y)P\). Negation reverses each quantifier and pushes inward: \(\sim(\forall x)(\exists y)(\forall z)P \Leftrightarrow (\exists x)(\forall y)(\exists z)\sim\!P\).
  2. Connectives, tautology, equivalence. \(\&,\text{ or},\sim,\Rightarrow,\Leftrightarrow\) via truth tables; \(A,B\) equivalent iff \(A\Leftrightarrow B\) is a tautology. De Morgan: \(\sim(P\text{ or }Q)\Leftrightarrow(\sim P)\,\&\,(\sim Q)\); \(\sim(P\Rightarrow Q)\Leftrightarrow P\,\&\,\sim Q\).
  3. Sets (extensionality). \(A=B\iff(\forall x)(x\in A\Leftrightarrow x\in B)\); \(A\subset B\iff(\forall x)(x\in A\Rightarrow x\in B)\); \(\{x:P(x)\}\); \(\varnothing\).
  4. Ordered pairs & relations. \(\langle x,y\rangle=\langle a,b\rangle\iff x=a\ \&\ y=b\); a relation is a set of pairs, with \(\operatorname{dom}\), \(\operatorname{range}\), \(R^{-1}\), and \(A\times B\).
  5. Functions. single-valued relation: \(\langle x,y\rangle,\langle x,z\rangle\in f\Rightarrow y=z\); \(f:A\to B\) injective / surjective / bijective.
  6. Composition & inverses. \((g\circ f)(x)=g(f(x))\), associative; a map has a two-sided inverse iff bijective. Bijections \(A\to A\) form a group under \(\circ\).
  7. Product sets. \(\prod_{i\in I}S_i=\{f:\operatorname{dom}f=I,\ f(i)\in S_i\}\); \(S^A\); \(2^S\cong\{0,1\}^S\).
  8. Duality (currying). \(F:A\times B\to C\ \leftrightarrow\ \varphi:A\to C^B\ \leftrightarrow\ \theta:B\to C^A\).
  9. Boolean operations. \(\bigcup,\bigcap,{}'\); De Morgan \(\big(\bigcap_i A_i\big)'=\bigcup_i A_i'\); preimages preserve all Boolean ops.
  10. Partitions & equivalence relations. Every equivalence relation is the relation of a partition (classes \(\bar x=\{y:y\sim x\}\)); \(g\) constant on fibers \(\iff g=\bar g\circ\pi\) (quotient universal property).

Beginner Level

Statements, quantifiers, and connectives

Intuition. Mathematics is written in sentences that are true or false. A sentence with a blank — "\(x<4\)" — becomes a statement only when you plug in a value or say how often it holds. "For every \(x\)" (\(\forall\)) and "for some \(x\)" (\(\exists\)) are the two ways to fill the blank, and their order matters: "every student has some locker" (\(\forall\exists\)) is far weaker than "some locker belongs to every student" (\(\exists\forall\)). Think of \(\forall\) as a challenge ("give me any \(x\)") and \(\exists\) as a promise ("I can produce a witness").

Formal definition. The connectives combine truth values:

\(P\)\(Q\)\(P\ \&\ Q\)\(P\text{ or }Q\)\(P\Rightarrow Q\)\(\sim P\)
TTTTTF
TFFTFF
FTFTTT
FFFFTT

"or" is always inclusive; \(P\Rightarrow Q\) is false only when \(P\) is true and \(Q\) false, so a false hypothesis makes the implication vacuously true.

Worked example. "\((\forall x)(x^2\ge 0)\)" is true over \(\mathbb{R}\) — squaring any real gives \(\ge 0\). But "\((\forall x)(x^2>0)\)" is false: \(x=0\) gives \(0>0\). A single counterexample destroys a \(\forall\)-statement; a single witness establishes an \(\exists\)-statement.

Sets, membership, and subsets

Intuition. A set is a collection regarded as one object; \(x\in A\) says \(x\) is a member. Two sets are equal exactly when they have the same members, so \(\{1,2,2\}=\{2,1\}\). The subsets of a small set, ordered by inclusion, form a lattice — the power set:

graph BT empty["∅"] --> a["{1}"] empty --> b["{2}"] empty --> c["{3}"] a --> ab["{1,2}"] a --> ac["{1,3}"] b --> ab b --> bc["{2,3}"] c --> ac c --> bc ab --> full["{1,2,3}"] ac --> full bc --> full

Each upward edge adds one element; the whole picture has \(2^3=8\) nodes because each element is independently in or out.

Worked example. Show \(\{x\in\mathbb{R}:x^2<9\}=(-3,3)\): \(x^2<9\iff|x|<3\iff-3<x<3\). A set defined by a property becomes a familiar interval — the bridge of analytic geometry.

Interactive — quick check on quantifiers, sets, and logic. Select an answer per question, then press Check. Each miss has an explanation you can open.

  1. What is the negation of \((\forall x)(\exists y)\,P(x,y)\)?

    Explanation

    Negation flips every quantifier (\(\forall\to\exists\), \(\exists\to\forall\)) and moves \(\sim\) to the matrix. So \(\forall\exists\) becomes \(\exists\forall\) with \(\sim\!P\) inside.

  2. Which statement is true over the real numbers?

    Explanation

    There is no least real, so \((\exists x)(\forall y)(x\le y)\) is false; but for each \(y\) we can pick \(x=y-1\), so \((\forall y)(\exists x)(x\le y)\) is true. And \(x=0\) breaks \(x^2>0\).

  3. A relation \(R\subset A\times B\) is a function exactly when:

    Explanation

    "Single-valued" is the defining property of a function; symmetry and \(R=R^{-1}\) are unrelated (those describe symmetric relations).

  4. How many subsets does a set with \(n\) elements have?

    Explanation

    Each element is independently in or out — a characteristic function in \(\{0,1\}^S\) — giving \(|2^S|=2^{n}\).

Exercises

1. Decide over \(\mathbb{R}\): (a) \((\exists x)(x^2=2)\); (b) \((\forall x)(x+1>x)\); (c) \((\exists x)(\forall y)(x\le y)\); (d) \((\forall y)(\exists x)(x\le y)\).

Solution

(a) True (\(x=\sqrt2\)). (b) True. (c) False — no least real; given \(x\), take \(y=x-1\). (d) True — pick \(x=y\). Parts (c) vs (d) show swapping quantifier order flips truth value.

2. Build the truth table of \(\sim(P\ \&\ Q)\) and compare it to \((\sim P)\text{ or }(\sim Q)\).

Solution
\(P\)\(Q\)\(P\&Q\)\(\sim(P\&Q)\)\((\sim P)\text{ or }(\sim Q)\)
TTTFF
TFFTT
FTFTT
FFFTT

The last two columns agree in every row — the forms are equivalent (De Morgan).

3. List all subsets of \(\{1,2,3\}\); how many, and why? State \(|2^S|\) for \(|S|=n\).

Solution

\(\varnothing,\{1\},\{2\},\{3\},\{1,2\},\{1,3\},\{2,3\},\{1,2,3\}\) — 8, since \(2^3=8\); in general \(|2^S|=2^n\) via characteristic functions.

4. Translate into symbols and negate: "every positive real has a positive square root."

Solution

Statement \((\forall x>0)(\exists y>0)(y^2=x)\); negation \((\exists x>0)(\forall y>0)(y^2\neq x)\).

5. Simplify \(\sim(P\Rightarrow Q)\) using only \(\&\) and \(\sim\), then negate "if it rains, the game is cancelled."

Solution

\(\sim(P\Rightarrow Q)\Leftrightarrow P\ \&\ (\sim Q)\); negation in words: "it rains and the game is not cancelled."

Intermediate Level

Negating quantifiers

Intuition. To disprove "everything works," exhibit one failure; to disprove "something works," show all fail. A negation flips every quantifier and slides inward — the workhorse of analysis: to say a sequence does not converge or a function is not continuous, mechanically negate its quantifier string and read off what to construct.

Formal definition. \(\sim(\forall x)P\Leftrightarrow(\exists x)\sim P\) and \(\sim(\exists x)P\Leftrightarrow(\forall x)\sim P\), applied left-to-right; inside, \(\sim(P\Rightarrow Q)\Leftrightarrow P\ \&\ \sim Q\).

Relations, functions, and composition

Intuition. A relation says which pairs correspond; a function insists each input has exactly one output. Composition chains functions — the arrow picture makes the bookkeeping obvious:

graph LR A["A (domain)"] -->|f| B["B"] B -->|g| C["C (codomain)"] A -->|"g ∘ f"| C

Formal definition. \(f:A\to B\) is injective iff \(f(x)=f(x')\Rightarrow x=x'\), surjective iff \(\operatorname{range}f=B\), bijective iff both; \(g\) is a right inverse when \(f\circ g=I_B\) (exists iff surjective), a left inverse when \(g\circ f=I_A\) (exists iff injective).

Worked example. \(g:\mathbb{R}\to[0,\infty)\), \(g(x)=x^2\) is surjective but not injective (\(g(2)=g(-2)=4\)). Right inverse \(h(y)=\sqrt y\): \((g\circ h)(y)=y\). But \((h\circ g)(-2)=2\neq-2\), so \(h\) is not a left inverse — matching the failure of injectivity.

Exercises

1. Negate "\((\forall\varepsilon>0)(\exists\delta>0)(\forall x)[\,|x-a|<\delta\Rightarrow|f(x)-f(a)|<\varepsilon\,]\)" (continuity at \(a\)) and read it in words.

Solution

\[(\exists\varepsilon>0)(\forall\delta>0)(\exists x)[\,|x-a|<\delta\ \&\ |f(x)-f(a)|\ge\varepsilon\,].\] "Some tolerance \(\varepsilon\) such that, however small \(\delta\), some \(x\) within \(\delta\) is thrown \(\ge\varepsilon\) away" — \(f\) is discontinuous at \(a\).

2. Prove \((A\cup B)'=A'\cap B'\).

Solution

\(x\in(A\cup B)'\iff\sim(x\in A\text{ or }x\in B)\iff(\sim x\in A)\ \&\ (\sim x\in B)\iff x\in A'\cap B'\).

3. For \(f:U\to V\), prove \(f^{-1}[B_1\cap B_2]=f^{-1}[B_1]\cap f^{-1}[B_2]\).

Solution

\(x\in f^{-1}[B_1\cap B_2]\iff f(x)\in B_1\ \&\ f(x)\in B_2\iff x\in f^{-1}[B_1]\cap f^{-1}[B_2]\).

4. Show images need not commute with intersection: find \(f\) with \(f[A\cap B]\subsetneq f[A]\cap f[B]\), and prove \(\subset\) always holds.

Solution

Always \(f[A\cap B]\subset f[A]\cap f[B]\). Strict: \(f(x)=x^2\), \(A=\{1\},B=\{-1\}\): \(f[A\cap B]=\varnothing\) but \(f[A]\cap f[B]=\{1\}\). Equality for all \(A,B\) iff \(f\) injective.

5. Prove: for \(f:A\to B\) with \(A\neq\varnothing\), \(f\) is injective iff it has a left inverse.

Solution

(\(\Leftarrow\)) \(g\circ f=I_A\Rightarrow f(x)=f(x')\Rightarrow x=g(f(x))=g(f(x'))=x'\). (\(\Rightarrow\)) fix \(a_0\); set \(g(y)=\) the unique preimage on \(\operatorname{range}f\), else \(a_0\); then \(g\circ f=I_A\).

Advanced Level

Set-theoretic foundations: pairs and inverses

Intuition. Everything — pairs, relations, functions, numbers — is built from sets. The payoff: structural facts (a bijection has a unique inverse; equivalence relations and partitions are the same thing) become short, rigorous set manipulations.

Formal definition. The Kuratowski pair is \(\langle a,b\rangle:=\{\{a\},\{a,b\}\}\). An equivalence relation \(\sim\) is reflexive, symmetric, transitive; a partition is a disjoint family of nonempty sets with union \(A\).

The fibering theorem and canonical decomposition

Worked example (fibering theorem). Every equivalence relation \(\sim\) on \(A\) is the relation of a partition.

Step 1. Classes \(\bar x=\{y:y\sim x\}\); reflexivity gives \(x\in\bar x\), so they cover \(A\).
Step 2. If \(x\in\bar a\cap\bar b\) then \(x\sim a,\ x\sim b\); for \(y\in\bar a\), transitivity gives \(y\sim x\sim b\), so \(y\in\bar b\). Thus \(\bar a\subset\bar b\), and symmetrically \(\bar a=\bar b\) — distinct classes are disjoint.
Step 3. So \(\{\bar x\}\) is a partition, and \(y\sim x\iff\) they share a class. \(\blacksquare\)

The universal property of the projection \(\pi:A\to A/\!\sim\), and the canonical factorization of any function as onto → bijection → inclusion:

Exercises

1. Verify the Kuratowski pair: \(\{\{a\},\{a,b\}\}=\{\{c\},\{c,d\}\}\iff a=c\ \&\ b=d\).

Solution

(\(\Leftarrow\)) clear. (\(\Rightarrow\)) If \(a=b\), LHS is a singleton, forcing \(c=d\) and \(a=c\). If \(a\neq b\), \(\{a\}\) is the unique 1-element member \(=\{c\}\Rightarrow a=c\), then \(\{a,b\}=\{a,d\}\Rightarrow b=d\).

2. If \(f\) has a right inverse \(h\) and a left inverse \(g\), then \(g=h\) and \(f\) is bijective with a unique inverse.

Solution

\(g=g\circ I_B=g\circ(f\circ h)=(g\circ f)\circ h=I_A\circ h=h\). Left inverse \(\Rightarrow\) injective, right \(\Rightarrow\) surjective; cancellation gives uniqueness.

3. Show \(\mathbb{Z}_p\) is the set of fibers of \(mEn\iff p\mid(m-n)\), and \(\bar m+\bar n:=\overline{m+n}\) is well defined.

Solution

\(E\) is an equivalence relation, so its classes partition \(\mathbb{Z}\), indexed by remainders \(\{0,\dots,p-1\}\). Well-defined: \(m'\in\bar m,n'\in\bar n\Rightarrow p\mid[(m'+n')-(m+n)]\).

4. For a relation \(R\), prove \(\operatorname{dom}(R^{-1})=\operatorname{range}(R)\) and \((R^{-1})^{-1}=R\).

Solution

\(x\in\operatorname{dom}(R^{-1})\iff(\exists y)\langle y,x\rangle\in R\iff x\in\operatorname{range}R\); and \(\langle x,y\rangle\in(R^{-1})^{-1}\iff\langle x,y\rangle\in R\).

5. Prove composition of relations is associative.

Solution

\(\langle a,c\rangle\in S\circ R\iff(\exists b)(\langle a,b\rangle\in R\ \&\ \langle b,c\rangle\in S)\). Since \(\exists\) and \(\&\) reassociate freely, \((T\circ S)\circ R\) and \(T\circ(S\circ R)\) unfold to the same condition.

Research Level

Currying as an adjunction

Intuition. The elementary "duality" of Chapter 0 is the shadow of a structural fact: in the category Set, "pair with \(B\)" and "raise to the power \(B\)" are adjoint. The three interconvertible maps \(F,\varphi,\theta\) are components of this adjunction:

graph LR F["F : A×B → C"] -->|"curry in A"| phi["φ : A → C^B"] phi -->|"uncurry"| F F -->|"fix y, vary x"| theta["θ : B → C^A"]

Development. The bijection \(\operatorname{Hom}(A\times B,C)\cong\operatorname{Hom}(A,C^B)\), natural in \(A\) and \(C\), says \((-)\times B\) is left adjoint to \((-)^B\): \((-\times B)\dashv(-)^B\). A category with finite products and all exponentials is cartesian closed — the semantics of the simply-typed \(\lambda\)-calculus, where currying is this isomorphism.

Quotients, universal properties, and coherence

Development. The projection \(\pi:A\to A/\!\sim\) is initial among maps collapsing \(\sim\); this lifts to \(\mathbb{Z}\twoheadrightarrow\mathbb{Z}_p\) and \(\mathbb{Z}\times(\mathbb{Z}\smallsetminus\{0\})\twoheadrightarrow\mathbb{Q}\), where the operations must also descend (a congruence). The associator \((A\times B)\times C\xrightarrow{\sim}A\times(B\times C)\) satisfies Mac Lane's coherence theorem, which is why "\(\mathbb{R}^3=\mathbb{R}^2\times\mathbb{R}\)" is harmless.

Exercises

1. Prove naturality of currying in \(A\): for \(u:A'\to A\), \(\Phi_{A'}(F\circ(u\times\mathrm{id}_B))=\Phi_A(F)\circ u\). Conclude \((-\times B)\dashv(-)^B\).

Solution

Evaluate at \(a',b\): both sides give \(F(u(a'),b)\). Naturality in \(A\) and \(C\) plus the bijection is the adjunction, with unit \(a\mapsto(b\mapsto\langle a,b\rangle)\).

2. Prove the exponential law \((C^B)^A\cong C^{A\times B}\) and identify it with currying.

Solution

\(\Psi(F)(a)(b)=F(a,b)\) with inverse \(\Psi^{-1}(\varphi)(a,b)=\varphi(a)(b)\) — mutually inverse, and exactly the currying isomorphism \(\operatorname{Hom}(A\times B,C)\cong\operatorname{Hom}(A,C^B)\).

3. State and prove the universal property of \(\mathbb{Q}\) as the field of fractions of \(\mathbb{Z}\).

Solution

Any injective ring hom \(\iota:\mathbb{Z}\to K\) (field) extends uniquely by \(\hat\iota(m/n)=\iota(m)\iota(n)^{-1}\); well-defined since \(mq=np\Rightarrow\iota(m)\iota(n)^{-1}=\iota(p)\iota(q)^{-1}\), and forced on every extension. This is localization at \(\mathbb{Z}\smallsetminus\{0\}\).

4. Give an explicit natural iso \(\alpha:(A\times B)\times C\to A\times(B\times C)\) and explain why coherence lets us write \(A\times B\times C\).

Solution

\(\alpha(\langle\langle a,b\rangle,c\rangle)=\langle a,\langle b,c\rangle\rangle\), a bijection natural in \(A,B,C\). Mac Lane coherence: all associativity diagrams commute, so unbracketed products are well defined up to canonical iso.

5. Show "\(\prod_{i\in I}S_i\neq\varnothing\) when all \(S_i\neq\varnothing\)" is equivalent to the Axiom of Choice.

Solution

An element of \(\prod S_i\) is a choice function \(f(i)\in S_i\); its existence for arbitrary nonempty \(S_i\) is exactly AC. Finite \(I\) is provable by induction; the content is entirely infinite (Gödel: consistent to assume; Cohen: consistent to deny).

Chapter 1 — Vector Spaces

"The calculus of several variables unites the calculus of one variable with the theory of vector spaces." Chapter 1 treats general (possibly infinite-dimensional) vector spaces: axioms, subspaces and span, linear transformations and their matrices, \(\operatorname{Hom}(V,W)\), quotient spaces, direct sums, and bilinearity. The recurring theme: the interesting maps of analysis (the derivative, the integral, evaluation) are exactly those that preserve the vector operations.

Key Concepts

  1. Axioms. Addition and scalar multiplication satisfying A1–A4 (abelian group) and S1–S4; consequences \(0\alpha=0\), \((-1)\alpha=-\alpha\). Standard example \(V=\mathbb{R}^A\).
  2. Subspace. nonempty \(W\subset V\) closed under \(+\) and scalars; \(\bigcap_i W_i\) is a subspace.
  3. Span. \(L(A)\) = smallest subspace containing \(A\); \(V\) is finite-dimensional if it has a finite spanning set.
  4. Linear map. \(T(x\alpha+y\beta)=xT\alpha+yT\beta\). Skeleton theorem: \(\alpha\mapsto L_\alpha\) is a bijection \(W^n\xrightarrow{\sim}\operatorname{Hom}(\mathbb{R}^n,W)\).
  5. Matrices. \(T:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m\) ↔ \(m\times n\) matrix (columns = skeleton), \(y_i=\sum_j t_{ij}x_j\).
  6. Kernel & range. \(N(T)=T^{-1}(0)\), \(R(T)=T[V]\); \(T\) injective iff \(N(T)=\{0\}\); bijective linear = isomorphism.
  7. \(\operatorname{Hom}(V,W)\). a vector space under pointwise ops; \(\operatorname{End}(V)\) an algebra under composition.
  8. Quotient. \(V/W\) of cosets \(\alpha+W\); \(\pi:V\to V/W\) linear, \(N(\pi)=W\); first isomorphism theorem \(V/N(T)\cong R(T)\).
  9. Direct sums. \(V=M\oplus N\iff M+N=V,\ M\cap N=\{0\}\); projections \(E^2=E\) correspond to decompositions.
  10. Bilinearity. \(\operatorname{Bil}(V\times W,X)\cong\operatorname{Hom}(V,\operatorname{Hom}(W,X))\); universal object = tensor product \(V\otimes W\).

Beginner Level

What is a vector space?

Intuition. Three very different-looking things obey the same algebra: geometric arrows (added by the parallelogram rule), coordinate tuples \(\langle x_1,x_2,x_3\rangle\), and functions \(f:A\to\mathbb{R}\) (added value-by-value). A "vector space" is their common denominator — a place where you can add and scale with the usual laws. Recognizing that polynomials, matrices, ODE solutions, and arrows are all vector spaces means every abstract theorem applies to all of them at once.

Formal definition. \(V\) is a real vector space if \(+\) and \(\cdot\) satisfy A1–A4 and S1–S4. \(W\subset V\) is a subspace if \(\alpha,\beta\in W\Rightarrow\alpha+\beta\in W\) and \(x\alpha\in W\). The subspaces of \(\mathbb{R}^3\), by dimension:

graph BT Z["{0} (dim 0)"] --> L["lines through 0 (dim 1)"] L --> P["planes through 0 (dim 2)"] P --> R["ℝ³ (dim 3)"]

The linear span \(L(A)=\{\sum_i x_i\alpha_i\}\) is the smallest subspace containing \(A\); if \(L(A)=V\), \(A\) spans \(V\).

Interactive — span of two vectors. Move the coefficients \(s,t\) to build the combination \(s\alpha+t\beta\) (orange). Change \(\beta\)'s second component: at \(\beta=(1,0.5)\) it becomes parallel to \(\alpha=(2,1)\), the determinant hits \(0\), and the span collapses from the whole plane to a single line.

Exercises

1. Show \(W=\{\langle x_1,x_2,x_3\rangle:x_1-2x_2+x_3=0\}\) is a subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^3\) and exhibit two spanning vectors.

Solution

\(0\in W\); closed under \(+\) and scalars since the defining equation is linear. Parametrize \(x_2=s,x_3=t,x_1=2s-t\): \(W=L(\langle2,1,0\rangle,\langle-1,0,1\rangle)\).

2. Show the solution set of \(x_1+x_2=1\) in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) is not a subspace.

Solution

\(\langle0,0\rangle\) is not in it — every subspace contains \(0\). It is an affine line, a translate of a subspace.

3. Can \(\langle2,0,1\rangle\) be written as a combination of \(\langle1,1,1\rangle\) and \(\langle0,1,-1\rangle\)?

Solution

Need \(\langle s,s+t,s-t\rangle=\langle2,0,1\rangle\): \(s=2,t=-2\) give \(s-t=4\neq1\). No — indeed \(2(2)-0-1=3\neq0\), off the plane.

4. Using only the axioms, prove \(0\alpha=0\) for every \(\alpha\).

Solution

\(0\alpha=(0+0)\alpha=0\alpha+0\alpha\) by S2; add \(-(0\alpha)\) to both sides to get \(0=0\alpha\).

5. Prove the intersection of two subspaces is a subspace; give a \(\mathbb{R}^2\) example where the union is not.

Solution

\(M\cap N\) contains \(0\) and is closed under both operations. The two coordinate axes are subspaces, but \(\langle1,0\rangle+\langle0,1\rangle=\langle1,1\rangle\notin\) axes.

Intermediate Level

Linear transformations and matrices

Intuition. A linear map is completely pinned down by its action on a spanning set — you never need it "everywhere," only on basis vectors. For \(\mathbb{R}^n\) that is the whole content of matrices: the \(j\)-th column records where \(\delta^j\) goes.

graph LR V["V (domain)"] -->|"T (linear)"| W["W (codomain)"] N["N(T) = T⁻¹(0) ⊆ V"] -.->|"collapses to"| zero["0 ∈ W"] R["R(T) = T[V] ⊆ W (image)"]

Formal definition. \(T(x\alpha+y\beta)=xT\alpha+yT\beta\); \(N(T)=T^{-1}(0)\), \(R(T)=T[V]\). Every linear \(T:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m\) is \(x\mapsto\mathbf{t}\,x\) with \(t_{ij}=(T\delta^j)_i\).

Worked example. \(T\langle x_1,x_2,x_3\rangle=\langle2x_1-x_2+x_3,\ x_1+3x_2-5x_3\rangle\) has skeleton \(T\delta^1=\langle2,1\rangle\), etc., so \(\mathbf{t}=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}2&-1&1\\1&3&-5\end{smallmatrix}\right)\); \(\dim N(T)=3-\operatorname{rank}=1\) (a line).

Exercises

1. Find the matrix and kernel of \(T\langle x_1,x_2,x_3\rangle=\langle x_1-x_2,\ x_2-x_3\rangle\).

Solution

\(\mathbf{t}=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}1&-1&0\\0&1&-1\end{smallmatrix}\right)\); kernel \(x_1=x_2=x_3\), i.e. \(L(\langle1,1,1\rangle)\).

2. For \(D\) on \(P_3\), find \(N(D),R(D)\) and check \(\dim N+\dim R=\dim P_3\).

Solution

\(N(D)=\) constants (\(\dim 1\)), \(R(D)=P_2\) (\(\dim 3\)); \(1+3=4\) (rank–nullity).

3. Prove a linear \(T:\mathbb{R}^n\to W\) is determined by \(T\delta^1,\dots,T\delta^n\).

Solution

\(x=\sum x_i\delta^i\Rightarrow T(x)=\sum x_iT(\delta^i)\); the skeleton bijection \(W^n\cong\operatorname{Hom}(\mathbb{R}^n,W)\).

4. Prove \(T\) is injective iff \(N(T)=\{0\}\).

Solution

Injective \(\Rightarrow N(T)=\{0\}\). Conversely \(T\alpha=T\beta\Rightarrow T(\alpha-\beta)=0\Rightarrow\alpha=\beta\).

5. Show \(\langle c_0,\dots,c_{n-1}\rangle\mapsto\sum c_it^i\) is an isomorphism \(\mathbb{R}^n\cong P_{n-1}\).

Solution

Linear, injective (a polynomial is zero iff all coefficients vanish), onto \(P_{n-1}\).

Advanced Level

Hom, quotients, and direct sums

Intuition. Once maps are objects, \(\operatorname{Hom}(V,W)\) is itself a vector space, and structural theorems become clean statements about maps: every linear map factors as a projection onto a quotient followed by an injection (first isomorphism theorem), and every subspace splits off via an idempotent projection.

Worked example (first isomorphism theorem). \(\bar T:V/N(T)\to R(T)\), \(\bar T(\alpha+N(T))=T\alpha\), is a well-defined isomorphism: well-defined (\(\alpha-\alpha'\in N(T)\Rightarrow T\alpha=T\alpha'\)), injective (\(T\alpha=0\Rightarrow\alpha\in N(T)\)), surjective onto \(R(T)\).

Exercises

1. Prove \(\operatorname{Hom}(V,W)\) is a vector space under pointwise operations.

Solution

\(S+T\) and \(cT\) are linear; axioms hold pointwise because they hold in \(W\); the zero is the zero map — a subspace of \(W^V\).

2. Prove \(V=M\oplus N\) iff every \(\gamma\) has a unique \(\gamma=\xi+\eta\), \(\xi\in M,\eta\in N\).

Solution

Existence \(\Leftrightarrow M+N=V\); uniqueness \(\Leftrightarrow M\cap N=\{0\}\) (a nonzero common vector gives two representations of itself).

3. For \(V=M\oplus N\), \(E(\gamma)=\xi\). Prove \(E\) linear, \(E^2=E\), \(R(E)=M\), \(N(E)=N\).

Solution

Uniqueness makes \(E\) well-defined and linear; \(\xi\in M\Rightarrow E\xi=\xi\), so \(E^2=E\), \(R(E)=M\); \(E\gamma=0\iff\gamma\in N\).

4. Given idempotent \(E\) (\(E^2=E\)), prove \(V=R(E)\oplus N(E)\).

Solution

\(\gamma=E\gamma+(\gamma-E\gamma)\) with \(E(\gamma-E\gamma)=0\); and \(R(E)\cap N(E)=\{0\}\) since \(\zeta=E\mu\Rightarrow\zeta=E\zeta=0\).

5. Prove rank–nullity via the first isomorphism theorem.

Solution

\(V/N(T)\cong R(T)\Rightarrow\dim R(T)=\dim V-\dim N(T)\), i.e. \(\dim N(T)+\dim R(T)=\dim V\).

Research Level

Bilinearity, tensor products, and exactness

Intuition. Bilinear maps (dot products, determinants, matrix multiplication, evaluation \(V\times V^*\to\mathbb{R}\)) are not linear on \(V\times W\), yet a single linear object governs them: the tensor product \(V\otimes W\), which converts bilinear maps out of \(V\times W\) into linear maps out of \(V\otimes W\). Linear algebra becomes the study of functors (\(\operatorname{Hom}\), \(\otimes\), duality) and the exact sequences they preserve.

Development. Universal property of \(\otimes\): \(\operatorname{Bil}(V\times W,X)\cong\operatorname{Hom}(V\otimes W,X)\). Exercises

1. State and prove the universal property characterizing \(V\otimes W\) up to unique isomorphism.

Solution

Every bilinear \(b:V\times W\to X\) factors uniquely as \(\tilde b\circ\otimes\). Two universal objects admit mutually inverse comparison maps (by the uniqueness clause), hence a canonical isomorphism.

2. Prove the tensor–hom adjunction.

Solution

Both sides are naturally \(\cong\operatorname{Bil}(U\times V,W)\): linear \(U\otimes V\to W\) = bilinear \(U\times V\to W\) = linear \(U\to\operatorname{Hom}(V,W)\) (curry the 2nd slot).

3. Prove \(0\to W\to V\to V/W\to0\) splits over a field, and relate a splitting to a projection.

Solution

Choose a complement \(N\) (extend a basis of \(W\)); \(\pi|_N:N\cong V/W\) gives a section, equivalently the idempotent projection onto \(W\). So \(V\cong W\oplus V/W\).

4. For which spaces does a Hamel basis exist, and why does analysis prefer topological bases for \(C([a,b])\)?

Solution

Every space has a Hamel basis iff AC (Zorn). But such a basis of \(C([a,b])\) is uncountable/non-constructive and only allows finite sums; analysis uses topological (Schauder / orthonormal) bases with infinite convergent series — needing a norm and completeness (Chs 3–5).

5. Show \(\operatorname{Hom}(-,X)\) is left exact, and the extra step making it exact over a field.

Solution

\(g^*\) injective and \(\ker f^*=\operatorname{im}g^*\) (a map killing \(\ker g\) factors through the quotient). Over a field the sequence splits, so \(f^*\) is onto too — every space is injective, so \(\operatorname{Hom}(-,X)\) is exact.

Chapter 2 — Finite-Dimensional Vector Spaces

Adding the single hypothesis "\(V\) has a finite spanning set" unlocks the classical toolkit: bases and dimension, coordinates, the dual space, matrices and change of basis, and the two great basis-independent invariants of an operator — its trace and determinant. The chapter closes with the diagonalization of a quadratic form, the algebraic seed of spectral theory.

Key Concepts

  1. Independence, basis, dimension. \(\sum x_i\alpha_i=0\Rightarrow x_i=0\); a basis is an independent spanning set; any two bases have the same size \(=\dim V\). \(\dim(M+N)=\dim M+\dim N-\dim(M\cap N)\).
  2. Coordinates. a basis gives \(L_\beta:\mathbb{R}^n\xrightarrow{\sim}V\); coordinate functionals \(\beta^i\).
  3. Dual space. \(V^*=\operatorname{Hom}(V,\mathbb{R})\), \(\dim V^*=\dim V\), dual basis \(\beta^i(\beta_j)=\delta^i_j\); evaluation \(V\to V^{**}\) is a natural isomorphism; transpose \(T^*:W^*\to V^*\).
  4. Matrix & similarity. change of basis \(A\mapsto P^{-1}AP\).
  5. Trace. \(\operatorname{tr}A=\sum a_{ii}\), \(\operatorname{tr}(AB)=\operatorname{tr}(BA)\Rightarrow\) similarity-invariant.
  6. Determinant. unique alternating multilinear function of the columns with \(\det I=1\); multiplicative; \(A\) invertible iff \(\det A\neq0\); \(|\det A|\) = volume factor.
  7. Eigen-theory. \(T\alpha=\lambda\alpha\); \(\chi_T(\lambda)=\det(\lambda I-A)\); eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues independent; diagonalizable iff a basis of eigenvectors exists.
  8. Quadratic forms. \(Q(x)=x^{\mathsf T}Ax\); Sylvester's law of inertia — the signature \((p,q)\) is a congruence invariant.

Intermediate Level

Dual space and change of basis

Intuition. For every space of vectors there is a shadow space of measurements — linear functionals (a coordinate, an average, an evaluation): the dual space \(V^*\). An operator has many matrices, one per basis, all related by conjugation \(A\mapsto P^{-1}AP\); the properties surviving conjugation (rank, trace, determinant, eigenvalues) truly belong to the operator.

1. Prove \(\det(AB)=\det A\det B\) implies \(\det\) is similarity-invariant and detects invertibility.

Intuition. Chapter 3 — The Differential Calculus

The analytic core of the book. Its decisive idea: the derivative of a map \(F:V\to W\) between normed spaces is a linear map \(dF_\alpha\) — the best linear approximation to the increment \(\Delta F_\alpha(\xi)=F(\alpha+\xi)-F(\alpha)\). Everything classical (partials, gradient, Jacobian, chain rule, mean-value theorem, implicit/inverse function theorems, Lagrange multipliers, Taylor) becomes a statement about this one linear object — valid even in infinite dimensions, where it is the "first variation" of the calculus of variations.

Key Concepts

  1. Norms. \(\|\alpha\|>0\ (\alpha\neq0)\), \(\|x\alpha\|=|x|\|\alpha\|\), \(\|\alpha+\beta\|\le\|\alpha\|+\|\beta\|\); norms \(\|\cdot\|_1,\|\cdot\|_2,\|\cdot\|_\infty\); open/closed sets, balls.
  2. Bounded linear maps. for linear \(T\): continuous \(\iff\) bounded \(\|T\xi\|\le C\|\xi\|\); operator norm \(\|T\|=\sup\|T\alpha\|/\|\alpha\|\).
  3. \(o\) and \(O\) classes. \(o\): \(\|g(\xi)\|/\|\xi\|\to0\); \(O\): bounded; the \(O\!\cdot\!o\)-theorem drives every differentiation rule.
  4. The differential. \(F\) differentiable at \(\alpha\) iff \(\Delta F_\alpha(\xi)=T(\xi)+o(\xi)\) for a bounded linear \(T=dF_\alpha\) (unique).
  5. Rules. \(d(F+G)=dF+dG\); \(d(FG)_\alpha=F(\alpha)dG_\alpha+dF_\alpha G(\alpha)\); a linear \(T\) has \(dT=T\).
  6. Chain rule. \(d(G\circ F)_\alpha=dG_{F(\alpha)}\circ dF_\alpha\).
  7. Coordinates. \(D_\xi F(\alpha)=dF_\alpha(\xi)\); Jacobian \([\partial F_i/\partial x_j]\); \(df_\alpha(\xi)=\nabla f(\alpha)\cdot\xi\); mean-value inequality \(\|F(\beta)-F(\alpha)\|\le(\sup\|dF\|)\|\beta-\alpha\|\).
  8. Inverse / implicit function theorems. \(dF_\alpha\) boundedly invertible \(\Rightarrow\) local diffeomorphism (proved via the contraction theorem, Ch 4).
  9. Lagrange multipliers. at a constrained extremum, \(df_\alpha=\sum_i\lambda_i\,d(g_i)_\alpha\).
  10. Taylor & critical points. \(F(\alpha+\xi)=F(\alpha)+dF_\alpha(\xi)+\tfrac12 d^2F_\alpha(\xi,\xi)+o(\|\xi\|^2)\); Hessian signature classifies critical points.

Advanced Level

The differential in normed spaces: definition, uniqueness, rules

Intuition. Loomis–Sternberg define the derivative with no coordinates: the unique bounded linear map whose error against the true increment is negligible (little-o). Uniqueness and every rule fall out of the algebra of the \(o\)/\(O\) classes.

Research Level

Fréchet vs Gâteaux, calculus of variations, Morse theory

Intuition. The abstract differential shines in infinite dimensions: the "derivative" of a functional \(J[y]=\int L\) is its first variation, and setting it to zero is the Euler–Lagrange equation — the whole calculus of variations is differential calculus on a Banach space.

graph LR J["J[y] = ∫ₐᵇ L(x, y, y′) dx"] -->|"first variation δJ = dJ_y(h) = 0 ∀ h"| EL["∂L/∂y − d/dx(∂L/∂y′) = 0"]

Development. Gâteaux (directional) is weaker than Fréchet (uniform). At a critical point, Taylor's second differential \(d^2F_\alpha\) (the Hessian) with signature \((p,q)\) puts \(F\) in Morse normal form \(F(\alpha)-\sum u_i^2+\sum v_j^2\); the index \(q\) classifies the critical point.

1. Give \(f\) Gâteaux but not Fréchet differentiable at \(0\).

2. Derive Euler–Lagrange from \(dJ_y(h)=0\).

Chapter 4 — Compactness and Completeness

The differential calculus of Chapter 3 wrote checks only completeness can cash: the inverse/implicit function theorems and (later) ODE existence all rest on passing to a limit. This chapter supplies the two topological pillars — compactness (every sequence has a convergent subsequence; continuous functions attain extrema and are uniformly continuous) and completeness (Cauchy sequences converge) — culminating in the elegant contraction-mapping fixed-point theorem.

Key Concepts

  1. Metric space. \(\rho\ge0\), \(\rho(x,y)=0\iff x=y\), symmetry, triangle inequality; a norm induces \(\rho(\alpha,\beta)=\|\alpha-\beta\|\).
  2. Convergence & closed sets. \(x_n\to x\iff\rho(x_n,x)\to0\); closed = contains sequential limits.
  3. Compactness. every sequence has a convergent subsequence; Heine–Borel: in \(\mathbb{R}^n\), compact \(\iff\) closed & bounded; continuous images of compacts are compact, so continuous reals attain max/min.
  4. Uniform continuity. continuous on a compact set \(\Rightarrow\) uniformly continuous.
  5. Equicontinuity — Arzelà–Ascoli. relatively compact in \(C(K)\) \(\iff\) uniformly bounded & equicontinuous.
  6. Completeness. Cauchy \(\Rightarrow\) convergent; Banach space = complete normed space; \(\mathbb{R}^n\), \(C(K)\) are Banach.
  7. Contraction theorem. a contraction (\(\rho(Kx,Ky)\le C\rho(x,y)\), \(C<1\)) on a complete space has a unique fixed point; \(x_n=K^n(x_0)\to x^*\).
  8. Complex numbers. \(\mathbb{C}=\mathbb{R}^2\) with \((a,b)(c,d)=(ac-bd,ad+bc)\), a complete field.

Chapter 5 — Scalar Product Spaces

Adding a scalar (inner) product imports the Euclidean notions of length, angle, and above all orthogonality. This yields orthogonal projection (best approximation), the adjoint of a transformation, and the crown jewel of finite-dimensional linear algebra — the spectral theorem: every self-adjoint operator has an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors. The compact-operator coda is the bridge to Fourier series and Sturm–Liouville theory.

Key Concepts

  1. Scalar product. symmetric, bilinear, positive definite; \(\|\alpha\|=\sqrt{(\alpha,\alpha)}\); Cauchy–Schwarz \(|(\alpha,\beta)|\le\|\alpha\|\|\beta\|\); \(\alpha\perp\beta\iff(\alpha,\beta)=0\).
  2. Orthonormal bases; Gram–Schmidt. \(\alpha=\sum(\alpha,e_i)e_i\); Parseval \(\|\alpha\|^2=\sum(\alpha,e_i)^2\); Bessel \(\le\).
  3. Orthogonal projection. \(V=M\oplus M^\perp\); \(P_M\alpha\) is the best approximation: \(\|\alpha-P_M\alpha\|=\min_{\mu\in M}\|\alpha-\mu\|\).
  4. Adjoint. \((T\alpha,\beta)=(\alpha,T^*\beta)\); self-adjoint \(T=T^*\); \((ST)^*=T^*S^*\).
  5. Spectral theorem. a self-adjoint operator has real eigenvalues and an orthonormal eigenbasis; \(A=Q\Lambda Q^{\mathsf T}\).
  6. Orthogonal transformations. \(T^*T=I\iff\) preserves the product \(\iff\) maps an orthonormal basis to one; the group \(O(n)\).
  7. Compact self-adjoint operators. orthonormal eigenvectors with eigenvalues \(\to0\) — the Hilbert-space spectral theorem behind Fourier/Sturm–Liouville.

Chapter 6 — Differential Equations

Here the abstract machinery pays off spectacularly. The fundamental existence–uniqueness theorem for \(d\alpha/dt=F(t,\alpha)\) is a one-line application of the contraction theorem to an integral equation. The linear theory uses Chapters 1–2 (solution spaces, the matrix exponential), and the chapter ends by recognizing the inverse of a boundary-value operator as a compact self-adjoint operator — from which the completeness of Fourier series drops out.

Key Concepts

  1. Initial-value problem. \(d\alpha/dt=F(t,\alpha)\), \(\alpha(t_0)=\alpha_0\); integral form \(f(t)=\alpha_0+\int_{t_0}^t F(s,f(s))\,ds\).
  2. Fundamental theorem. \(F\) locally uniformly Lipschitz in \(\alpha\) \(\Rightarrow\) unique local solution, extending to a maximal one; global if the Lipschitz bound is integrable.
  3. Reduction of order. an \(n\)th-order equation becomes a first-order system on \(W^n\).
  4. Linear equations. homogeneous solutions form a vector space; constant \(A\): \(\alpha(t)=e^{(t-t_0)A}\alpha_0\).
  5. \(n\)th-order linear. solution space has dimension \(n\); Wronskian; characteristic equation.
  6. Inhomogeneous. variation of parameters / Duhamel: \(\alpha=e^{(t-t_0)A}\alpha_0+\int e^{(t-s)A}\beta(s)\,ds\).
  7. Boundary-value problems. \(Ly=f\) inverts to \(y=\int G(x,s)f(s)\,ds\) (Green's function).
  8. Fourier series. a Sturm–Liouville Green's operator is compact self-adjoint, so its eigenfunctions form an orthonormal basis.

Chapter 7 — Multilinear Functionals

An algebraic interlude that builds the machinery the integral calculus on manifolds runs on: multilinear and especially alternating functionals. The star is the exterior (Grassmann) algebra \(\bigwedge V^*\), whose degree-\(n\) part is one-dimensional and is the determinant, and whose elements become the differential forms one integrates.

Key Concepts

  1. Multilinear functionals. linear in each argument; tensor product \(\omega\otimes\eta\); \(\dim=n^k\).
  2. Permutations & sign. \(\operatorname{sgn}\sigma=(-1)^{\#\text{transpositions}}\), a homomorphism \(S_k\to\{\pm1\}\).
  3. Alternating tensors. change sign under transpositions (vanish on repeats); \(\bigwedge^k V^*\), \(\dim=\binom nk\).
  4. Wedge product. associative, graded-commutative \(\alpha\wedge\beta=(-1)^{k\ell}\beta\wedge\alpha\); basis \(e^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge e^{i_k}\) (\(i_1<\cdots<i_k\)).
  5. Determinant. \(\bigwedge^n V^*\) is one-dimensional; \(\det T\) = scalar of \(\bigwedge^n T\).
  6. Exterior algebra. \(\bigwedge V^*=\bigoplus_k\bigwedge^k V^*\), total dimension \(2^n\).
  7. Star operator. on an oriented scalar-product space, \(*:\bigwedge^k V^*\to\bigwedge^{n-k}V^*\), \(**=(-1)^{k(n-k)}\).

Chapter 8 — Integration

Loomis–Sternberg build the Riemann integral axiomatically from content (Jordan measure). From rectangles and paved sets come inner/outer content, contented (Jordan-measurable) sets with the criterion "\(A\) contented \(\iff\partial A\) has content zero," the \(|\det|\) behavior under linear maps, the integral of a contented function, Fubini, the change-of-variables formula, and — as a problem set — the Fourier transform.

Key Concepts

  1. Rectangles & paved sets. \(\square_a^b=\{x:a^i\le x^i<b^i\}\); content \(\mu(\square_a^b)=\prod(b^i-a^i)\), additive.
  2. Contented sets. \(A\) contented iff \(\mu_*(A)=\mu^*(A)\); criterion: \(\partial A\) has content zero.
  3. Linear distortions. \(\mu(T(A))=|\det T|\,\mu(A)\).
  4. The integral. Riemann sums; integrable iff discontinuities have (content/measure) zero.
  5. Change of variables. \(\int_{g(A)}f=\int_A(f\circ g)\,|\det dg|\).
  6. Fubini. \(\int_{A\times B}f=\int_A\!\big(\int_B f\,dy\big)dx=\int_B\!\big(\int_A f\,dx\big)dy\).
  7. Absolutely integrable functions. improper/unbounded-domain integrals (\(L^1\)).
  8. Fourier transform. \(\hat f(\xi)=\int f(x)e^{-2\pi i x\cdot\xi}dx\); Plancherel \(\|\hat f\|_2=\|f\|_2\).

Chapter 9 — Differentiable Manifolds

The book's hardest chapter: it replaces flat vector spaces with manifolds — spaces that only locally look like \(\mathbb{R}^n\). The new objects are the tangent space (the linearization of a curved space at a point), vector fields and their flows, the Lie derivative, differential 1-forms, and the Riemann metric that restores lengths and angles.

Key Concepts

  1. Atlas & manifold. charts \(\varphi_\alpha:U_\alpha\to\mathbb{R}^n\) with \(C^\infty\) transition maps.
  2. Tangent space. \(T_pM\) = derivations (or velocity classes of curves), \(\dim=n\), basis \(\partial/\partial x^i\).
  3. Tangent bundle & vector fields. \(X=\sum X^i\partial_i\), a smooth section of \(TM\).
  4. Flows. integral curves \(\dot\gamma=X_\gamma\) assemble into a one-parameter group \(\varphi_t\).
  5. Lie bracket & derivative. \([X,Y]=XY-YX\); \(\mathcal L_XT=\frac{d}{dt}\big|_0(\varphi_{-t})_*T\), \(\mathcal L_XY=[X,Y]\).
  6. Differential forms. \(\omega=\sum\omega_i\,dx^i\); \(df=\sum\partial_if\,dx^i\); forms pull back.
  7. Riemann metric. \(g=\sum g_{ij}\,dx^i\,dx^j\) (positive definite): lengths, angles, volume.

Intuition. A manifold looks flat up close but may be curved globally — the Earth's surface is locally a flat map yet globally a sphere. Chapter 10 — The Integral Calculus on Manifolds

To integrate on a manifold there is a problem invisible in \(\mathbb{R}^n\): no canonical coordinates, so the integrand must transform correctly under changes of chart. The solutions are partitions of unity (which glue local integrals into a global one) and densities (integrands carrying the \(|\det|\) Jacobian factor, so they integrate unambiguously on any — even non-orientable — manifold). The volume density of a metric and the divergence theorem are the payoff.

Key Concepts

  1. Partitions of unity. smooth \(\rho_\alpha\ge0\), \(\sum\rho_\alpha=1\), \(\operatorname{supp}\rho_\alpha\subset U_\alpha\); \(\int_M f=\sum_\alpha\int_{U_\alpha}\rho_\alpha f\).

Chapter 11 — Exterior Calculus

The summit of the book's development of the calculus. The exterior algebra of Chapter 7, spread over a manifold, becomes the algebra of differential forms; the single operator \(d\) unifies gradient, curl, and divergence; and one theorem — Stokes' theorem \(\int_M d\omega=\int_{\partial M}\omega\) — contains the fundamental theorem of calculus, Green's, Kelvin–Stokes', and Gauss's theorems as special cases.

Key Concepts

  1. Differential forms. \(\omega=\sum_{|I|=k}\omega_I\,dx^I\), the graded algebra \(\Omega(M)\) under \(\wedge\).
  2. Exterior derivative. \(d:\Omega^k\to\Omega^{k+1}\), antiderivation, \(d^2=0\), natural: \(d(F^*\omega)=F^*(d\omega)\).
  3. Orientation & integration. an \(n\)-form integrates over an oriented \(n\)-manifold.
  4. Stokes' theorem. \(\int_M d\omega=\int_{\partial M}\omega\).
  5. Classical specializations. FTC (\(n=1\)), Green (\(n=2\)), Kelvin–Stokes (surface), divergence (\(n=3\)).
  6. Lie derivative of a form. Cartan \(\mathcal L_X=d\iota_X+\iota_X d\).
  7. Vector-analysis dictionary. in \(\mathbb{R}^3\): \(d=\)grad/curl/div by degree; \(d^2=0\) is \(\operatorname{curl}\operatorname{grad}=0\), \(\operatorname{div}\operatorname{curl}=0\).

Chapter 13 — Classical Mechanics

The book closes by showing that everything it built — manifolds, forms, the exterior derivative, the Lie derivative — is the natural language of mechanics. Positions live on a configuration manifold \(Q\); momenta on the cotangent bundle \(T^*Q\), the phase space, which carries a canonical symplectic form. Newton's laws become the flow of a Hamiltonian vector field, conservation laws become Noether symmetries, and the subject is revealed as symplectic geometry.

Key Concepts

  1. Configuration & phase space. \((q,\dot q)\in TQ\) (Lagrangian), \((q,p)\in T^*Q\) (Hamiltonian); tautological 1-form \(\theta=\sum p_i\,dq^i\).
  2. Lagrangian mechanics. Hamilton's principle \(\delta\!\int L\,dt=0\Rightarrow\) Euler–Lagrange \(\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q^i}=\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}\).
  3. Legendre & Hamilton's equations. \(p_i=\partial L/\partial\dot q^i\), \(H=\sum p_i\dot q^i-L\); \(\dot q^i=\partial H/\partial p_i\), \(\dot p_i=-\partial H/\partial q^i\).
  4. Symplectic form. \(\omega=\sum dq^i\wedge dp_i\); \(\iota_{X_H}\omega=dH\).
  5. Poisson bracket. \(\{f,g\}=\sum(\partial_qf\partial_pg-\partial_pf\partial_qg)\); \(\dot f=\{f,H\}\).
  6. Noether. continuous symmetry \(\Rightarrow\) conserved quantity (translations→momentum, rotations→angular momentum, time→energy).
  7. Liouville. the Hamiltonian flow preserves phase volume \(\omega^n/n!\).
  8. Applications. Kepler/central force, rigid-body (Euler), small oscillations, canonical transformations / Hamilton–Jacobi.

Intuition. The Legendre transform trades velocities for momenta, converting Euler–Lagrange into Hamilton's symmetric first-order pair. \(H\) is usually the energy, and conservation laws become transparent: cyclic coordinate \(\Rightarrow\) conserved momentum; time-independent \(H\Rightarrow\) conserved energy.

Exercises

1. \(H\) and Hamilton's equations for the harmonic oscillator; phase orbits?

Solution

\(H=\tfrac{p^2}{2m}+\tfrac12kq^2\); \(\dot q=p/m,\dot p=-kq\); orbits are ellipses.

2. Time-independent \(H\Rightarrow H\) conserved.

Solution

\(\dfrac{dH}{dt}=\{H,H\}+\partial_tH=0\).

3. Cyclic coordinate \(\Rightarrow\) conserved momentum.

Solution

\(\dot p_k=-\partial H/\partial q^k=0\).

4. Reduce Kepler to a radial problem.

Solution

\(\ell=mr^2\dot\theta\) conserved; \(E=\tfrac12m\dot r^2+V_{\text{eff}}(r)\).

5. Hamilton's equations for free fall \(H=\tfrac{p^2}{2m}+mgq\).

Solution

\(\ddot q=-g\); \(q=q_0+\tfrac{p_0}mt-\tfrac12gt^2\).

Advanced Level

Symplectic geometry: the modern formulation

Intuition. Hamilton's equations say the flow is the Hamiltonian vector field \(X_H\) of the symplectic form \(\omega\). The Poisson bracket makes observables a Lie algebra; Liouville (volume preservation) and Noether (symmetry ↔ conservation) become one-line consequences of \(d\omega=0\) and \(\mathcal L_{X_H}\omega=0\).

graph LR T["phase space T*Q"] -->|"ω = Σ dq ∧ dp (closed, nondegenerate)"| sympl["symplectic manifold"] H["Hamiltonian H"] -->|"ι_{X_H} ω = dH"| XH["Hamiltonian field X_H"] XH -->|"flow"| pres["preserves ω ⇒ Liouville volume"] sym["symmetry: ℒ_X L = 0"] -->|"Noether"| cons["conserved quantity"]

Worked example (Liouville). \(\mathcal L_{X_H}\omega=d\iota_{X_H}\omega+\iota_{X_H}d\omega=d(dH)+0=0\), so the flow preserves \(\omega\) and phase volume.

Exercises

1. Derive Hamilton's equations from \(\iota_{X_H}\omega=dH\).

Solution

\(\iota_{X_H}\omega=\sum(a^i dp_i-b_i dq^i)=dH\Rightarrow a^i=\partial_{p_i}H,b_i=-\partial_{q^i}H\).

2. Prove \(\dot f=\{f,H\}\).

Solution

Chain rule + Hamilton's equations give \(\sum(\partial_qf\partial_pH-\partial_pf\partial_qH)\).

3. Poisson bracket satisfies the Jacobi identity.

Solution

\(X_{\{f,g\}}=[X_f,X_g]\); inherit Jacobi from the Lie bracket.

4. Prove Noether's theorem.

Solution

Symmetry \(\frac{dL}{ds}=0\) + Euler–Lagrange \(\Rightarrow\frac{d}{dt}(\sum p_i\frac{dq^i}{ds})=0\).

5. Hamiltonian flows are symplectomorphisms.

Solution

\(\frac{d}{dt}\varphi_t^*\omega=\varphi_t^*\mathcal L_{X_H}\omega=0\).

Research Level

Integrable systems, Hamilton–Jacobi, and quantization

Intuition. Symplectic geometry organizes the deepest facts of mechanics. Integrable systems confine motion to invariant tori; KAM theory explains why most tori survive perturbations (solar-system stability). The Hamilton–Jacobi equation reveals mechanics as the geometric-optics limit of a wave theory — the bridge to quantum mechanics.

Worked example (action–angle). For \(H=\tfrac{p^2}{2m}+\tfrac12m\omega^2q^2\), the action \(I=\tfrac1{2\pi}\oint p\,dq=E/\omega\), \(H=\omega I\), \(\dot\varphi=\omega\); Bohr–Sommerfeld \(I=n\hbar\) gives \(E_n=n\hbar\omega\).

Exercises

1. State Arnold–Liouville and define action–angle variables.

Solution

\(n\) commuting integrals \(\Rightarrow\) level sets are tori with \((I,\varphi)\), \(\dot I=0\), \(\dot\varphi=\partial H/\partial I\).

2. Derive the Hamilton–Jacobi equation.

Solution

Seek \(S\) with new \(K=0\): \(H(q,\partial S/\partial q)+\partial S/\partial t=0\).

3. State the KAM theorem and its significance.

Solution

Most Diophantine tori survive small perturbation — stability of nearly-integrable systems.

4. Define the moment map and state Marsden–Weinstein reduction.

Solution

\(\mu:M\to\mathfrak g^*\), \(d\langle\mu,\xi\rangle=\iota_{X_\xi}\omega\); \(\mu^{-1}(0)/G\) is symplectic.

5. Poisson bracket vs quantum commutator.

Solution

\(\{f,g\}\mapsto\tfrac1{i\hbar}[\hat f,\hat g]\); \([\hat q,\hat p]=i\hbar\); classical limit \(\hbar\to0\).
