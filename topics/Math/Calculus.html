Beginner Level

Limits

Intuition: A limit asks: "what value is \(f(x)\) heading toward as \(x\) approaches \(c\)?" — regardless of what happens exactly at \(c\). It is the tool that turns "almost" into precise mathematics, and all of calculus is built on it.

Formal definition: \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L\) means: \(\forall \varepsilon > 0\ \exists \delta > 0\) such that \(0 < |x - c| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon\). (Note \(0 < |x - c|\): the point \(c\) itself is excluded — limits don't care about \(f(c)\).)

Interactive: a limit existing where the function doesn't. \(f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}\) is undefined at \(x = 1\) (hole), yet the limit there is 2 — the ε-δ game still works because the definition never asks about \(x = 1\) itself.

Step-by-step: \(\lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}\)

Step 1: Try the numeric table
Why? Before algebra, see what the values suggest.

Both sides squeeze toward 2.

Step 2: Factor and cancel
Why is cancelling allowed? Because the limit only involves \(x \neq 1\), where \(x - 1 \neq 0\):
\[\frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} = \frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x - 1} = x + 1 \quad (x \neq 1)\]
Step 3: Evaluate the simplified expression \[\lim_{x \to 1}(x + 1) = 2\]
Limit laws (valid when the individual limits exist): the limit of a sum/product/quotient is the sum/product/quotient of limits (quotient: denominator limit ≠ 0); constants pull out; and the squeeze theorem: if \(g \le f \le h\) near \(c\) and \(g, h\) share the limit \(L\), then \(f \to L\) too.
Real-World Applications:
  • Instantaneous speed: a speedometer reading is the limit of average speed \(\frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}\) as the stopwatch interval shrinks — the derivative below is exactly this limit.
  • 0/0 forms: physical formulas often collapse to \(\frac{0}{0}\) at interesting points (e.g., relativistic formulas as \(v \to c\)); limits extract the meaningful value.

Exercises:

  1. Compute \(\lim_{x \to 3} \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}\).
    Solution Factor: \(\frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3} = x + 3 \to 6\).
  2. Use the squeeze theorem on \(\lim_{x \to 0} x^2 \sin(1/x)\).
    Solution \(-x^2 \le x^2\sin(1/x) \le x^2\) and both bounds → 0, so the limit is 0.

Intermediate Level

Derivatives

Intuition: the derivative \(f'(a)\) is the slope of the tangent line at \(a\) — the limit of secant slopes (see the animated secant widget in Functions § Differentiable):

\[f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h}\]

Interactive: a function and its derivative, together. The dashed green curve is the numerically computed \(f'\); the tangent line at the slider point has exactly that slope. Notice: \(f' = 0\) exactly where \(f\) has flat spots.

Differentiation rules as algorithms

Power rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}x^n = nx^{n-1}\). Worked: \(\frac{d}{dx}x^5 = 5x^4\).
Product rule: \((uv)' = u'v + uv'\). Worked: \(\frac{d}{dx}\big(x^2 \sin x\big) = 2x\sin x + x^2\cos x\).
Quotient rule: \(\left(\frac{u}{v}\right)' = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2}\). Worked: \(\frac{d}{dx}\frac{\sin x}{x} = \frac{x\cos x - \sin x}{x^2}\).
Chain rule (algorithm): to differentiate \(f(g(x))\):
1. Identify outer \(f\) and inner \(g\).
2. Differentiate the outer function evaluated at the inner: \(f'(g(x))\).
3. Multiply by the inner derivative: \(f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\).

Worked: \(\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x^2)\): outer \(\sin\), inner \(x^2\) → \(\cos(x^2) \cdot 2x\).

Geometric Picture (chain rule)

Composition is a two-stage stretching machine: \(g\) stretches the input axis locally by the factor \(g'(x)\), then \(f\) stretches \(g\)'s output axis by \(f'(g(x))\). A tiny interval of width \(dx\) comes out with width \(f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \, dx\) — local stretch factors multiply. That product is the whole chain rule.

Step-by-step application: related rates (the sliding ladder)

A 5 m ladder leans on a wall; its base slides away at 1 m/s. How fast does the top slide down when the base is 3 m out?

Step 1: Relate the quantities — Pythagoras: \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\).
Step 2: Differentiate the relation in time (chain rule!): \(2x\frac{dx}{dt} + 2y\frac{dy}{dt} = 0\).
Step 3: Substitute the snapshot — \(x = 3 \Rightarrow y = 4\), \(\frac{dx}{dt} = 1\):
\[2(3)(1) + 2(4)\frac{dy}{dt} = 0 \Rightarrow \frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{3}{4} \text{ m/s}\] The top slides down at 0.75 m/s.
Step-by-step application: optimization (best fence)

100 m of fence, one side along a river (no fence needed). Maximize the rectangular area.

Step 1: Model — sides \(x\) (perpendicular, ×2) and \(y\): \(2x + y = 100\), area \(A = xy = x(100 - 2x)\).
Step 2: Differentiate and find critical points — \(A'(x) = 100 - 4x = 0 \Rightarrow x = 25\).
Step 3: Check it's a max — \(A''(x) = -4 < 0\) (concave — see Convexity) ✓. Also check endpoints: \(A(0) = A(50) = 0\).
Answer: \(x = 25\) m, \(y = 50\) m, \(A = 1250\) m².

Exercises:

  1. Differentiate \(f(x) = e^{3x}\cos(2x)\).
    Solution Product + chain: \(f'(x) = 3e^{3x}\cos(2x) - 2e^{3x}\sin(2x) = e^{3x}(3\cos 2x - 2\sin 2x)\).
  2. A spherical balloon inflates at 10 cm³/s. How fast does the radius grow when \(r = 5\) cm?
    Solution \(V = \frac43\pi r^3 \Rightarrow \frac{dV}{dt} = 4\pi r^2 \frac{dr}{dt}\), so \(\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{10}{4\pi \cdot 25} = \frac{1}{10\pi} \approx 0.032\) cm/s.

Integrals & the Fundamental Theorem

Intuition: the definite integral \(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) is the signed area under the graph — computed as the limit of sums of thin rectangles (Riemann sums).

Interactive: Riemann sums for \(\int_0^1 x^2 dx = \frac13\). Increase \(n\) and watch the rectangle sum converge; switch the sample point (left/mid/right) and compare error sizes.

Step-by-step: Riemann sum by hand, n = 4 (right endpoints)

Step 1: Split \([0,1]\) into 4 pieces of width \(\Delta x = \frac14\); right endpoints \(\frac14, \frac12, \frac34, 1\).
Step 2: Sum height × width: \[\left(\tfrac{1}{16} + \tfrac{4}{16} + \tfrac{9}{16} + \tfrac{16}{16}\right)\cdot\tfrac14 = \tfrac{30}{64} = 0.46875\] Step 3: Compare: exact is \(1/3 \approx 0.3333\) — right endpoints overshoot an increasing function. The widget shows the error melting as \(n\) grows.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC)

Part 1: if \(F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt\) (the accumulation function), then \(F'(x) = f(x)\).
Part 2: \(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)\) for any antiderivative \(F\) of \(f\).

Geometric meaning: as \(x\) creeps right by \(dx\), the accumulated area grows by a sliver of width \(dx\) and height \(f(x)\) — so the accumulation's growth rate is the curve's height. Derivative and integral are inverse operations.

Integration techniques as algorithms

u-substitution (algorithm):
1. Choose \(u = g(x)\) so that \(du = g'(x)dx\) appears in the integrand.
2. Rewrite everything in terms of \(u\) and \(du\).
3. Integrate in \(u\); substitute back.

Worked: \(\int 2x\cos(x^2)\,dx\): let \(u = x^2\), \(du = 2x\,dx\) → \(\int \cos u\, du = \sin u + C = \sin(x^2) + C\).

Geometric Picture (u-substitution)

A change of variables is a rubber-sheet re-gridding of the x-axis: \(u = g(x)\) stretches the axis non-uniformly, and the factor \(du = g'(x)\,dx\) is exactly the local stretch factor. The area is carried over unchanged — same area, more convenient shape.

Integration by parts (algorithm): \(\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du\).
1. Pick \(u\) by LIATE (Log, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trig, Exponential) — the earlier in the list, the better a \(u\).
2. Everything else is \(dv\); compute \(du\) and \(v\).
3. Apply the formula; the new integral should be simpler.

Worked: \(\int x e^x dx\): \(u = x\) (algebraic), \(dv = e^x dx\) → \(du = dx\), \(v = e^x\): \[\int x e^x dx = x e^x - \int e^x dx = (x - 1)e^x + C\]

Geometric Picture (integration by parts)

Plot the curve as points \((u, v)\) and draw the rectangle with corners \((0,0)\) and \((u, v)\): its area \(u \cdot v\) splits into the region swept horizontally (\(\int v\,du\)) plus the region swept vertically (\(\int u\,dv\)). The formula \(\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du\) is literally this picture — the rectangle minus one region equals the other.

Real-World Applications:
  • Distance from speed: odometer = integral of speedometer.
  • Work: \(W = \int F(x)\,dx\) — pumping water, stretching springs.
  • Probability: probabilities are areas under density curves (see the normal distribution).

Exercises:

  1. Evaluate \(\int_0^{\pi/2} \sin x \, dx\).
    Solution Antiderivative \(-\cos x\): \((-\cos\frac{\pi}{2}) - (-\cos 0) = 0 + 1 = 1\).
  2. Evaluate \(\int x \ln x \, dx\).
    Solution Parts with \(u = \ln x\), \(dv = x\,dx\): \(\frac{x^2}{2}\ln x - \int \frac{x}{2}dx = \frac{x^2}{2}\ln x - \frac{x^2}{4} + C\).

Advanced Level

Partial Derivatives & the Gradient

Intuition: for \(f(x, y)\) — a landscape of heights — the gradient \(\nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right)\) is the arrow pointing straight uphill, with length equal to the steepness. It is always perpendicular to the level curves (contour lines).

Interactive: gradient fields with level curves. Arrows are \(\nabla f\); thin closed curves are level sets. See the arrows cross contour lines at right angles, pointing uphill.

Step-by-step: gradient and directional derivative of \(f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2\) at \((1, 2)\)

Step 1: Partials — treat the other variable as constant: \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2x\), \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2y\).
Step 2: Evaluate — \(\nabla f(1,2) = (2, 4)\).
Step 3: Directional derivative along unit \(\mathbf{u} = \left(\frac{3}{5}, \frac{4}{5}\right)\): \[D_{\mathbf u}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf u = 2\cdot\tfrac35 + 4\cdot\tfrac45 = \tfrac{22}{5} = 4.4\] Steepest possible rate at this point: \(\|\nabla f\| = \sqrt{20} \approx 4.47\) — along the gradient itself.

Green's Theorem

Green's theorem converts a line integral around a closed curve into a double integral over the enclosed region:

\[\oint_{\partial D}(P\,dx + Q\,dy) \;=\; \iint_D\left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA\]

  • \(\partial D\): the boundary curve of region \(D\), traversed counterclockwise
  • \(P, Q\): the components of the vector field \(\mathbf F = (P, Q)\)
  • Left side: the circulation — how much the field pushes you around the loop
  • Right side: the curl \(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\) (local spin) summed over the inside

The unifying geometric sentence: add up a local quantity (here: curl) over the inside, and everything cancels except at the boundary — the boundary remembers the sum.

Interactive: watch the proof idea. The field \(\mathbf F = (-y, x)\) rotates. Green dots circulate around the boundary square. Raise the subdivision slider: every small cell gets its own circulating loop — and on every interior edge, the two neighboring loops run in opposite directions (drawn faded), cancelling exactly. Only the outer boundary survives. Toggle the curl heatmap: here curl \(= 2\) everywhere, so circulation \(= 2 \times\) Area.

Step-by-step: verify Green's theorem for \(\mathbf F = (-y, x)\) on the unit square \([0,1]^2\)

Right side (double integral): \(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 1 - (-1) = 2\), so \(\iint_D 2\,dA = 2 \times 1 = 2\).

Left side (line integral), edge by edge counterclockwise:
Bottom \((t, 0), t: 0 \to 1\): \(\int P\,dx = \int_0^1 (-0)\,dt = 0\).
Right \((1, t)\): \(\int Q\,dy = \int_0^1 1\,dt = 1\).
Top \((1-t, 1)\): \(\int P\,dx = \int_0^1 (-1)(-dt) = 1\).
Left \((0, 1-t)\): \(\int Q\,dy = \int_0^1 0 \cdot(-dt) = 0\).
Total: \(0 + 1 + 1 + 0 = 2\). ✓ Both sides equal 2.
Real-World Applications:
  • Planimeter: the mechanical device that measures area by tracing a shape's boundary — Green's theorem in brass.
  • Area formula: \(\text{Area} = \frac12\oint(x\,dy - y\,dx)\) — how GIS software computes polygon areas from vertex lists.
  • Fluid dynamics: circulation around a wing section relates to lift (Kutta–Joukowski).

Divergence (Gauss) Theorem

The 2D flux form (what the canvas shows): flux of \(\mathbf F\) out through the boundary = total divergence inside:

\[\oint_{\partial D} \mathbf F \cdot \mathbf n \, ds \;=\; \iint_D \nabla\!\cdot\!\mathbf F \, dA\]

Full 3D statement (divergence theorem): \(\oiint_{\partial V} \mathbf F \cdot d\mathbf S = \iiint_V \nabla\!\cdot\!\mathbf F \, dV\) — what flows out through a closed surface equals what is produced inside. Same sentence again: sources summed inside; the boundary remembers.

Interactive: field 1 is \(\mathbf F = (x, y)\) — a source at the origin (\(\nabla\!\cdot\!\mathbf F = 2\) everywhere): particles stream outward and pierce the circle; each crossing flashes. Field 2 is the rotation \((-y, x)\) — divergence-free: particles circulate and nothing ever crosses. Change the radius and check the readout: flux \(= 2\pi r^2\) exactly.

Step-by-step: verify for \(\mathbf F = (x, y)\) on the unit disk

Right side: \(\nabla\!\cdot\!\mathbf F = \frac{\partial x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial y}{\partial y} = 2\), so \(\iint_D 2\,dA = 2\pi\).

Left side: on the unit circle the outward normal is \(\mathbf n = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta)\) and \(\mathbf F = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta)\), so \(\mathbf F \cdot \mathbf n = \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1\): \[\oint \mathbf F \cdot \mathbf n\, ds = \int_0^{2\pi} 1 \cdot d\theta = 2\pi \; ✓\]
Real-World Applications:
  • Maxwell's equations: \(\nabla\!\cdot\!\mathbf E = \rho/\varepsilon_0\) + divergence theorem = Gauss's law: charge inside determines the field through any surrounding surface.
  • Conservation laws: mass/energy balance in any control volume of a fluid simulation.
  • Incompressible flow: water satisfies \(\nabla\!\cdot\!\mathbf v = 0\) — the widget's field 2.

Stokes' Theorem

The full 3D statement (Stokes' theorem): for an oriented surface \(S\) with boundary curve \(\partial S\),

\[\oint_{\partial S} \mathbf F \cdot d\mathbf r \;=\; \iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf F)\cdot d\mathbf S\]

Green's theorem is exactly the flat special case (surface lying in the plane, curl reduced to its vertical component). The proof idea is identical: tile the surface with tiny loops; interior edges cancel; the boundary remembers the total curl.

Interactive (projected 3D): a disk-shaped surface tilted by angle α, drawn in orthographic projection. Dots circulate along its boundary; the small loops are the curl sampled on the surface. As you tilt, the projected area — and with it the flux of curl \(\iint(\nabla\times\mathbf F)\cdot d\mathbf S\) — scales with \(\cos\alpha\), while the circulation around the boundary always matches it.

Real-World Applications:
  • Faraday's law: \(\oint \mathbf E \cdot d\mathbf r = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}\) — the EMF around a wire loop equals the changing magnetic flux through any surface it bounds; Stokes' theorem is why the choice of surface doesn't matter.
  • Vorticity in fluids: circulation around a contour measures the total vorticity (curl of velocity) it encloses — the mathematics of smoke rings and hurricanes.

Exercises:

  1. Use Green's theorem to compute the area of the ellipse \(x = a\cos t,\ y = b\sin t\).
    Solution \(\text{Area} = \frac12\oint(x\,dy - y\,dx) = \frac12\int_0^{2\pi}(ab\cos^2 t + ab\sin^2 t)\,dt = \frac12 \cdot 2\pi ab = \pi ab\).
  2. For \(\mathbf F = (y, -x)\) (clockwise rotation), what is the circulation counterclockwise around any region of area \(A\)?
    Solution Curl \(= \frac{\partial(-x)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial y}{\partial y} = -2\), so circulation \(= -2A\) (the field opposes counterclockwise travel).

Research Level

Differential Forms: One Theorem to Rule Them All

The FTC, Green, Gauss, and Stokes are all shadows of the generalized Stokes theorem:

\[\int_M d\omega = \int_{\partial M} \omega\]

where \(\omega\) is a differential \(k\)-form on an oriented \((k{+}1)\)-manifold \(M\) and \(d\) is the exterior derivative. Choose \(M\) an interval and \(\omega = F\) a 0-form → FTC. Choose \(M\) a plane region and \(\omega = P\,dx + Q\,dy\) → Green. Choose \(M\) a solid and \(\omega\) a 2-form → Gauss. In every case, the same sentence holds: the boundary remembers the sum of the local derivative inside.

de Rham Cohomology

Which fields with zero curl are gradients? On a plane with a hole (like \(\mathbf F = \frac{(-y, x)}{x^2+y^2}\), curl-free but with circulation \(2\pi\) around the origin), the answer detects the hole itself. de Rham cohomology turns "closed but not exact forms" into a precise algebraic invariant of the shape of space — differential equations detecting topology. This bridge (de Rham's theorem) is a cornerstone of modern geometry and mathematical physics (gauge theory, string theory).
