Beginner Level

Vectors

Intuition: a vector is an arrow: it has a length and a direction, but no fixed starting point. Forces, velocities, and displacements are all vectors — and they add tip-to-tail.

Formal definitions for \(\mathbf u = (u_1, u_2)\), \(\mathbf v = (v_1, v_2)\):

  • Sum: \(\mathbf u + \mathbf v = (u_1 + v_1,\ u_2 + v_2)\) — the parallelogram diagonal
  • Norm (length): \(\|\mathbf u\| = \sqrt{u_1^2 + u_2^2}\)
  • Dot product: \(\mathbf u \cdot \mathbf v = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 = \|\mathbf u\|\|\mathbf v\|\cos\theta\)

The dot product's sign is geometric: positive → the vectors point broadly the same way (angle \(< 90°\)); zero → perpendicular; negative → opposing.

Interactive: vector addition & dot product. Set each vector's length and angle; the dashed parallelogram construction gives \(\mathbf u + \mathbf v\).

Step-by-step: for \(\mathbf u = (3, 4)\), \(\mathbf v = (2, -1)\)

Sum: \(\mathbf u + \mathbf v = (3+2,\ 4-1) = (5, 3)\).
Norm: \(\|\mathbf u\| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{25} = 5\).
Dot product: \(\mathbf u \cdot \mathbf v = 3(2) + 4(-1) = 2 > 0\) → angle under 90°.
The angle itself: \(\cos\theta = \frac{2}{5\sqrt5} \approx 0.179 \Rightarrow \theta \approx 79.7°\).
Real-World Applications:
  • Forces: two ropes pulling a load — the net force is the parallelogram diagonal in the widget.
  • Velocity composition: a plane's airspeed plus the wind vector = ground velocity.
  • Similarity search: recommender systems measure how "aligned" two preference vectors are with exactly the widget's \(\cos\theta\) (cosine similarity).

Exercises:

  1. Find a vector perpendicular to \((4, 3)\) with the same length.
    Solution Swap and negate one component: \((-3, 4)\) (or \((3, -4)\)). Check: \(4(-3) + 3(4) = 0\) ✓, norm 5 ✓.
  2. For which \(k\) are \((k, 2)\) and \((3, -6)\) perpendicular?
    Solution \(3k - 12 = 0 \Rightarrow k = 4\).

Intermediate Level

Matrices as Transformations

Intuition: a matrix is not a table of numbers — it is a machine that moves space. The 2×2 matrix \(A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}\) sends the basis arrow \(\mathbf e_1 = (1,0)\) to column one \((a, c)\) and \(\mathbf e_2 = (0,1)\) to column two \((b, d)\); everything else follows by linearity — the whole grid deforms accordingly.

Matrix–vector product:

\[A\mathbf x = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = x\begin{pmatrix} a \\ c \end{pmatrix} + y\begin{pmatrix} b \\ d \end{pmatrix}\]

— a weighted mix of the columns. Matrix–matrix multiplication is composition: apply one machine, then the other.

Interactive: watch space deform. Press Play to animate from the identity \(I\) to your matrix \(A\): the grid bends, the letter-F rides along, and the colored arrows are the images of \(\mathbf e_1, \mathbf e_2\) (the columns!). The readout tracks \(\det A\) — the factor by which areas scale (negative = orientation flips).

Step-by-step: multiply \(A\mathbf x\) for \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\), \(\mathbf x = (1, 2)\)

Columns view (what the widget draws): \(A\mathbf x = 1\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix} + 2\begin{pmatrix}1\\3\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}4\\6\end{pmatrix}\) — one of column 1 plus two of column 2.

Rows view (dot products): row 1: \(2(1) + 1(2) = 4\); row 2: \(0(1) + 3(2) = 6\). Same answer, two mental models.
Real-World Applications:
  • Computer graphics: every rotation, scale, and shear of a sprite or 3D model is a matrix; composing transformations = multiplying matrices (the GPU does this billions of times per frame).
  • Robotics: joint angles → end-effector position via chains of transformation matrices.
  • Coordinate changes: converting between camera, world, and screen coordinates.

Solving Systems: Gaussian Elimination

The algorithm (Gaussian elimination) solves \(A\mathbf x = \mathbf b\):

1. Forward elimination: for each column, use the pivot row to zero out every entry below the pivot (row operation \(R_j \leftarrow R_j - k R_{pivot}\)).
2. Pivoting: if a pivot is 0 (or tiny), swap in a lower row with the largest entry in that column first.
3. Back substitution: the triangular system solves from the bottom row upward.

Geometric Picture

Each equation is a line (2D) or plane (3D); the solution is where they all meet. A row operation replaces one line with a new line through the same intersection point — any combination of two equations is still satisfied by their common solution. Elimination is a sequence of rotations of lines about the fixed solution, until each line is axis-parallel and the solution can be read off. The solution never moves; only its description simplifies.

Interactive (geometric): rotate lines about the solution. System: \(x + y = 5\), \(2x - y = 4\) (solution \((3,2)\), from Equations & Inequalities). Each Step animates one row operation — watch the pulsing solution dot stay perfectly still.

Step-by-step: full 3×3 elimination — solve \[\begin{cases} x + y + z = 6 \\ 2x - y + z = 3 \\ x + 2y - z = 2 \end{cases}\]
Step 1: \(R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1\) (kill the 2 in column 1): \[\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & | & 6 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & | & -9 \\ 1 & 2 & -1 & | & 2 \end{pmatrix}\] Step 2: \(R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_1\): \[\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & | & 6 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & | & -9 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 & | & -4 \end{pmatrix}\] Step 3: \(R_3 \leftarrow R_3 + \frac13 R_2\) (kill the 1 in column 2): \[\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & | & 6 \\ 0 & -3 & -1 & | & -9 \\ 0 & 0 & -\tfrac73 & | & -7 \end{pmatrix}\] Step 4: Back substitution (bottom-up):
\(-\frac73 z = -7 \Rightarrow z = 3\);   \(-3y - 3 = -9 \Rightarrow y = 2\);   \(x + 2 + 3 = 6 \Rightarrow x = 1\).
Solution: \((1, 2, 3)\) ✓ (verify in all three originals).

Interactive (algebraic): the same eliminations as row operations. Step through and watch the matrix become triangular; the pivot entry is bracketed.

Real-World Applications:
  • Circuit analysis: Kirchhoff's laws produce linear systems in the unknown currents — every SPICE simulation eliminates thousands of them.
  • Structural engineering: forces in a truss form a system with one equation per joint.
  • Least squares: fitting a model to data reduces to the "normal equations" — a linear system (see Numerical Methods).

Exercises:

  1. Solve by elimination: \(2x + y = 7\), \(x - 3y = -7\).
    Solution \(R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - \frac12 R_1\): \(-\frac72 y = -\frac{21}{2} \Rightarrow y = 3\); then \(2x + 3 = 7 \Rightarrow x = 2\).
  2. What happens geometrically when elimination hits a row \(0 = 0\)? A row \(0 = 5\)?
    Solution \(0 = 0\): the lines/planes coincide along a whole line → infinitely many solutions. \(0 = 5\): contradiction, the lines are parallel and never meet → no solution.

Advanced Level

Determinants & Eigenvalues

Determinant, geometrically: \(\det A\) is the factor by which \(A\) scales areas (2D) or volumes (3D); a negative sign means orientation flips; \(\det A = 0\) means space is squashed flat (no inverse!). The matrix widget above shows this live.

2×2: \(\det\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d\end{pmatrix} = ad - bc\).

3×3 by cofactor expansion (worked): \[\det\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = 2\det\begin{pmatrix}3 & 1\\ 1 & 2\end{pmatrix} - 1\det\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\ 0 & 2\end{pmatrix} + 0 = 2(6-1) - 1(2-0) = 8\]

Eigenvalues, geometrically: a matrix mostly turns vectors. An eigenvector is a direction the matrix refuses to turn — it only stretches it, by the factor \(\lambda\):

\[A\mathbf v = \lambda \mathbf v, \quad \mathbf v \neq 0\]

Interactive: hunt the eigenvectors. Press Play: the unit vector \(\mathbf x\) (blue) sweeps a full turn while \(A\mathbf x\) (green) follows. The moment they align, the pair flashes — you've found an eigendirection; the readout shows the stretch factor \(\lambda\).

Step-by-step: eigenvalues of \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\)

Step 1: Characteristic polynomial — eigenvalues make \(A - \lambda I\) singular: \[\det(A - \lambda I) = \det\begin{pmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1 \\ 1 & 2-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = (2-\lambda)^2 - 1 = 0\] Step 2: Solve — \((2-\lambda)^2 = 1 \Rightarrow \lambda = 1\) or \(\lambda = 3\).

Step 3: Eigenvectors — for \(\lambda = 3\): \((A - 3I)\mathbf v = 0 \Rightarrow -v_1 + v_2 = 0 \Rightarrow \mathbf v = (1, 1)\). For \(\lambda = 1\): \(v_1 + v_2 = 0 \Rightarrow \mathbf v = (1, -1)\).

Step 4: Verify — \(A(1,1)^T = (3,3)^T = 3(1,1)^T\) ✓ — exactly the directions where the widget flashes.

Geometric Picture (power iteration)

Apply \(A\) repeatedly to any starting vector and normalize each time: every application turns the vector a little further toward the dominant eigendirection (largest \(|\lambda|\)), because that component gets amplified the most. After a few iterations the vector settles onto it — this is exactly how PageRank finds the web's dominant eigenvector.

Real-World Applications:
  • PageRank: the web's link matrix has a dominant eigenvector — its entries are the page ranks.
  • PCA: the eigenvectors of a data covariance matrix are the directions of maximal variance — dimensionality reduction in one theorem.
  • Vibration modes: a bridge's resonant frequencies are eigenvalues; engineers keep them away from footstep and wind frequencies.
  • Quantum mechanics: measurable quantities are eigenvalues of operators (see Functions § Research).

Exercises:

  1. Find the eigenvalues of \(\begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\).
    Solution \((4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = (\lambda-5)(\lambda-2)\) → \(\lambda = 5, 2\).
  2. Why can't a 2×2 rotation matrix (by 90°) have real eigenvalues?
    Solution A 90° rotation turns every vector — no direction survives unturned, so no real eigenvector exists. (Algebra agrees: \(\lambda^2 + 1 = 0\) has only imaginary roots \(\pm i\).)

Research Level

Abstract Vector Spaces

Everything above survives when "arrows" are replaced by anything obeying the vector axioms: polynomials, audio signals, quantum states, solutions of linear ODEs. Functions form infinite-dimensional vector spaces; the dot product generalizes to inner products like \(\langle f, g\rangle = \int f g\,dx\), and Fourier series are just coordinates in an orthonormal basis.

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

Every matrix — square or not — factors as \(A = U\Sigma V^T\) with \(U, V\) orthogonal (rotations/reflections) and \(\Sigma\) diagonal (pure stretching): every linear map is rotate → stretch → rotate. Applications: image compression (keep the top-\(k\) singular values), noise reduction, the pseudo-inverse for least squares, and recommender systems (latent factors).

Random Matrix Theory

The eigenvalues of large random matrices obey universal laws (Wigner's semicircle, Marchenko–Pastur) that appear in nuclear physics, wireless communication, and the statistics of neural network training — an active research frontier connecting probability, physics, and numerical analysis.
